Big picture

Torts is asking:

Did someone wrongfully hurt another person, property, reputation, privacy, business, or legal rights?

The first move on the MBE is always:

What tort are they testing?

Because the answer changes depending on the tort.

Think of torts as different “buckets”:

Intentional torts — someone meant to do the act.
Negligence — someone was careless.
Strict liability — someone can be liable even if careful.
Products liability — bad product caused harm.
Defamation/privacy/business torts — words, identity, business, or legal process caused harm.
1. Intentional torts

Intentional torts usually require:

Act + intent + causation

Intent means either:

The person wanted the result, or
The person knew the result was substantially certain.

Memory:

Intent = purpose or substantial certainty.

Example

Maya pulls a chair away right as Leo is sitting down because she wants him to fall.

That is intentional.

If Maya says:

“I only meant it as a joke,”

that does not erase intent. She intended the contact/fall.

2. Transferred intent

Transferred intent means:

If defendant intends one intentional tort against one person, but hits another person or causes a different covered tort, intent transfers.

Usually applies to:

battery
assault
false imprisonment
trespass to land
trespass to chattels

Usually not ordinary IIED.

Example

Maya throws a marker intending to hit Leo.

Leo ducks.

The marker hits Noah.

Maya’s intent transfers to Noah.

Memory:

Bad intent can follow the harm.

3. Battery

Battery is:

Intentional harmful or offensive contact.

Important:

Battery requires intent to cause contact, not intent to injure.

Example

Maya intentionally shoves Leo lightly as a joke.

Leo falls and breaks his wrist.

Battery.

Maya did not need to intend the broken wrist. She intended the contact.

Contact can be indirect

If Maya knocks a tray out of Leo’s hands, that can be battery.

Why?

Because the tray is connected to Leo’s person.

Memory:

Battery = intentional bad touch.

4. Assault

Assault is:

Intentionally causing someone to reasonably anticipate imminent harmful or offensive contact.

No actual touching needed.

Example

Maya raises a metal bat like she is about to swing at Leo.

Leo sees it and expects to be hit.

Assault.

If she actually hits him, then it is assault and battery.

Memory:

Assault = “I think I’m about to be touched.”

No fear required

Leo does not need to be terrified.

He just needs to anticipate imminent contact.

Future threats usually do not count

If Maya texts:

“I’ll hit you next month,”

that is usually not assault because it is not imminent.

Memory:

Assault must be now-ish.

5. Intentional infliction of emotional distress

IIED requires:

Extreme and outrageous conduct that intentionally or recklessly causes severe emotional distress.

This is not normal rudeness.

Example

Maya falsely tells Leo that his child was seriously injured, just to emotionally destroy him.

That can be IIED.

But if Maya merely says:

“You are annoying,”

that is usually not enough.

Memory:

IIED = outrageous + severe distress.

Special people/situations

Conduct is more likely outrageous if defendant has power over plaintiff.

Examples:

employer over employee
police officer over citizen
school official over student
targeting someone known to be especially vulnerable

Memory:

Power imbalance can make conduct more outrageous.

6. False imprisonment

False imprisonment is:

Intentional confinement within a limited area, and plaintiff is aware of it.

Example

Maya locks Leo inside a storage closet and blocks the only safe exit.

False imprisonment.

No false imprisonment if there is a safe known exit

If Leo knows there is an unlocked door right behind him, he is not confined.

But he does not have to use a dangerous exit.

Memory:

False imprisonment = trapped with no reasonable safe way out.

Threats can confine

If Maya says:

“Stay in this office or I’ll hurt your sister,”

that can be confinement by duress.

7. Consent

Consent is a defense.

If plaintiff consented, defendant usually is not liable.

Example

Leo agrees to spar with Maya in a boxing class.

Maya lightly punches him within the rules.

No battery.

But if Maya pulls out a chair and hits Leo with it, that exceeds consent.

Memory:

Consent works only within its scope.

Consent can be revoked

If Leo says:

“Stop. I’m done.”

Maya cannot keep hitting him.

8. Self-defense

A person may use reasonable force to protect against an immediate unlawful threat.

Example

Leo raises his fist and steps toward Maya.

Maya pushes him away.

Likely privileged.

Memory:

Self-defense = necessary + proportional + immediate.

No revenge

If Leo hits Maya and walks away, Maya cannot chase him later and hit him back.

That is retaliation, not self-defense.

Memory:

Self-defense stops danger. It does not punish the past.

Deadly force

Deadly force requires a reasonable belief of:

death
serious bodily harm
rape

Usually, you cannot use deadly force for a minor shove.

9. Defense of others

Same as self-defense, but you are protecting another person.

Example

Maya reasonably sees Leo about to hit Noah.

Maya pushes Leo away.

That may be defense of others.

Key:

Defendant must reasonably believe the third person had a right to self-defense.

Memory:

Defense of others = reasonable rescue from immediate force.

10. Defense of property

You can use reasonable, nondeadly force to protect property.

Example

Maya sees Leo grabbing her laptop from her desk.

She pulls it back and blocks him.

Maybe okay.

But she cannot use deadly force just to protect the laptop.

Memory:

Property is not worth deadly force.

No force to regain land

If someone wrongfully occupies your land, you usually must use legal process.

You cannot just beat them up to get the land back.

11. Merchant’s privilege

A merchant can briefly detain someone suspected of theft if the merchant acts reasonably.

Need:

Reasonable belief of theft or nonpayment.
Detention near the store.
Reasonable manner.
Reasonable time.
Example

A bookstore employee sees Leo hide a book in his coat.

The employee stops him near the exit and asks him to wait briefly.

Likely okay.

But locking him in a basement for four hours is not okay.

Memory:

Merchant can reasonably stop, not bully.

12. Trespass to chattels

Chattel = personal property.

Trespass to chattels is:

Intentional interference with someone’s personal property.

It is the “less serious” property tort.

Example

Maya grabs Leo’s tablet and uses it for 10 minutes without permission.

Trespass to chattels.

If there is only minor interference, plaintiff usually needs actual damages unless there was dispossession.

Memory:

Trespass to chattels = messing with someone’s stuff.

13. Conversion

Conversion is the serious version.

It means:

Defendant seriously interferes with personal property so much that defendant must pay full value.

Example

Maya takes Leo’s laptop, sells it, and keeps the money.

Conversion.

Leo can recover the full value of the laptop.

Memory:

Conversion = forced sale.

Difference from trespass to chattels

Small interference = trespass to chattels.
Big interference = conversion.

14. Trespass to land

Trespass to land is:

Intentional physical invasion of another’s land.

Important:

Defendant does not need to know the land belongs to someone else.

Example

Maya walks onto a garden because she honestly thinks it is public property.

It is actually Leo’s private land.

Trespass.

Why?

She intended to enter that physical place.

Memory:

Mistake about ownership is not a defense.

But accidental entry is different

Maya is playing frisbee in a park.

She aims at Noah, but the frisbee accidentally curves into Leo’s yard.

That may not be trespass if she did not intend the frisbee to enter that land.

Maybe negligence, but not intentional trespass.

Memory:

Mistaken entry = trespass. Accidental entry = maybe negligence.

15. Necessity

Necessity is a defense to property torts.

It applies when defendant enters or uses someone’s property to avoid serious harm.

Private necessity

Private necessity protects a small/private interest.

The defendant may enter, but must pay actual damage.

Example

Maya is caught in a dangerous snowstorm and enters Leo’s empty cabin to avoid freezing.

She may be privileged to enter.

But if she breaks the door, she pays for the door.

Memory:

Private necessity = allowed in, but pay actual damage.

Public necessity

Public necessity protects many people/the public.

Usually no liability if reasonable.

Example

A firefighter destroys a shed to stop a wildfire from reaching a neighborhood.

No liability if reasonable.

Memory:

Public necessity = public emergency, no pay.

16. Private nuisance

Private nuisance is:

Substantial and unreasonable interference with another person’s use and enjoyment of land.

This is about making land unpleasant or unusable.

Example

Maya runs a machine behind Leo’s house every night. It shakes the walls and keeps everyone awake.

Private nuisance.

Memory:

Private nuisance = messes with my land enjoyment.

Need substantial interference

Tiny annoyance is not enough.

It must bother a normal reasonable person.

If Leo is unusually sensitive, he cannot recover unless an ordinary person would also be bothered.

17. Public nuisance

Public nuisance is:

Unreasonable interference with a right common to the public.

Example

Maya blocks the only public walking path to a city park.

That harms the public.

A private person can sue only if they suffer harm different in kind from the public.

Example

Everyone has to walk around the blocked path.

That is general public harm.

But if Leo’s wheelchair-accessible entrance is uniquely blocked and he suffers a special injury, he may sue.

Memory:

Public nuisance = harms community rights. Private plaintiff needs special harm.

18. Negligence

Negligence is careless harm.

Elements:

Duty
Breach
Causation
Damages

Memory:

Duty = should defendant be careful?
Breach = was defendant careless?
Causation = did carelessness cause harm?
Damages = real injury/property loss?

Example

Maya leaves a sharp tool on a crowded gym floor.

Leo steps on it and is injured.

Negligence.

19. Duty

Usually, people owe reasonable care to foreseeable plaintiffs.

Example

Maya throws heavy boxes off a balcony.

People walking below are foreseeable plaintiffs.

She owes them care.

Memory:

Foreseeable plaintiff = duty.

No general duty to rescue

Usually, you do not have to help.

Example

Maya sees Leo faint on a sidewalk.

She usually has no duty to help.

But there are exceptions.

20. Exceptions to no-duty-to-rescue

A duty to act can arise if:

Defendant starts helping.
Defendant created the danger.
Contract requires help.
Defendant has authority/control.
Special relationship.
Statute requires action.
Example: started helping

Maya starts carrying Leo after he falls.

She must act reasonably.

Example: created danger

Maya accidentally knocks Leo into a hole.

She must take reasonable steps to help.

Memory:

No duty to start, but once you start or create danger, act reasonably.

21. Standard of care

The general standard is:

Reasonable person under the circumstances.

This is objective.

Example

Maya says:

“I am naturally careless.”

Does not matter.

She is judged like a reasonable person.

Memory:

Reasonable person, not this person’s best effort.

Physical disability

Physical disability matters.

A blind person is judged like a reasonable blind person.

Mental disability

Mental/emotional disability usually does not lower the standard.

Children

Children are judged like reasonable children of similar age, intelligence, and experience.

But if a child does an adult dangerous activity, adult standard.

Example

A 13-year-old rides a small scooter on the sidewalk: child standard.

A 13-year-old operates a forklift: adult standard.

22. Professionals

Professionals must act like ordinary professionals in the same field.

Example

An architect designs a building support system.

The jury usually needs expert testimony to know if the architect acted properly.

But if the architect used cardboard as support beams, no expert needed.

Why?

A normal person can see that is obviously careless.

Memory:

Professional negligence usually needs expert testimony unless the mistake is obvious.

23. Land possessors

Land possessor duties depend on who entered the land.

Traditional categories:

Trespasser
Licensee
Invitee
Trespasser

A trespasser enters without permission.

Usually landowner must not intentionally/willfully/wantonly hurt them.

No deadly traps.

Example

Maya sets a hidden blade trap in her shed for intruders.

Not allowed.

Memory:

Trespasser gets little protection, but no traps.

Known trespasser

If the landowner knows people often trespass, must warn of hidden dangerous artificial conditions.

Licensee

A licensee has permission, but not for business.

Usually social guest.

Duty:

Warn of known hidden dangers.

Example

Maya invites Leo to dinner.

She knows a stair is loose.

She should warn him.

Memory:

Licensee = warn about hidden dangers you know.

Invitee

Invitee enters for business or public purpose.

Duty:

Reasonable care, including inspection.

Example

A café must reasonably inspect for spills where customers walk.

Memory:

Invitee = inspect and make safe.

24. Landlord liability

Once tenant takes possession, tenant usually becomes responsible.

But landlord remains liable for:

common areas
hidden dangers landlord failed to disclose
premises leased for public use
negligent repairs
hazards landlord agreed to repair
Example

Landlord controls the apartment building lobby.

A broken lobby tile injures a visitor.

Landlord may be liable.

Memory:

Landlord liable where landlord still controls or promised/caused the problem.

25. Breach

Breach means defendant failed to meet the required standard of care.

Ask:

What would a reasonable person do?

Example

Maya leaves boiling oil on the edge of a table at a children’s party.

Likely breach.

Memory:

High risk + serious harm + easy fix = breach.

26. Custom

Custom is evidence of reasonable care, but not conclusive.

Example

Every warehouse stacks boxes too high.

A box falls and injures Leo.

The warehouse cannot automatically win by saying:

“Everyone does it.”

The whole industry may be careless.

Memory:

Custom helps, but custom does not control.

For professionals, custom matters much more.

27. Negligence per se

Negligence per se means a statute supplies the standard of care.

Need:

Statute imposes a specific duty.
Defendant violated it.
Plaintiff is in protected class.
Harm is the type the statute was meant to prevent.
Example

A city rule says restaurants must cover open floor drains to prevent customer falls.

Restaurant leaves one uncovered.

Customer falls in.

Negligence per se likely.

Memory:

Statute violation = duty/breach shortcut, if plaintiff and harm match statute.

Trap

If a statute is about tax paperwork, and plaintiff slipped on a floor, the statute does not match the harm.

No negligence per se.

28. Res ipsa loquitur

Res ipsa means:

The accident itself suggests negligence.

Use when there is no direct evidence, but the thing usually does not happen without negligence.

Need usually:

Accident ordinarily does not happen without negligence.
Instrumentality was in defendant’s control.
Plaintiff did not cause it.
Example

Leo is walking past a bakery and a rolling pin falls from the bakery’s upstairs window onto him.

Rolling pins do not normally fall from windows unless someone was careless.

Res ipsa.

Memory:

Res ipsa = “this probably happened because someone was careless.”

It does not mean plaintiff automatically wins.

It usually just gets plaintiff to the jury.

29. Causation

There are two types:

Actual cause
Proximate cause
Actual cause

Ask:

But for defendant’s conduct, would the harm have happened?

Example

Maya leaves a cord across the hallway.

Leo trips.

But for the cord, he would not have fallen.

Actual cause.

Proximate cause

Ask:

Was the harm foreseeable and within the risk?

Example

Leaving a cord across a hallway creates a risk that someone trips.

Leo tripping is foreseeable.

Proximate cause.

Memory:

Actual cause = did it cause it?
Proximate cause = is it fair to blame defendant?

30. Intervening and superseding causes

An intervening cause happens after defendant’s negligence.

If foreseeable, it does not cut off liability.

If unforeseeable and extraordinary, it may be superseding and cut off liability.

Foreseeable intervening cause

Maya injures Leo.

A nurse later makes a treatment mistake.

Maya can still be liable because medical mistakes are foreseeable.

Superseding cause

Maya leaves a harmless-looking box on the sidewalk.

A stranger intentionally turns it into a dangerous trap.

That criminal/intentional act may cut off liability unless Maya had a duty to prevent that kind of act.

Memory:

Foreseeable intervening = still liable. Unforeseeable superseding = maybe cuts off.

31. Eggshell plaintiff

Defendant takes plaintiff as found.

The type of harm must be foreseeable, but the extent does not.

Example

Maya lightly bumps Leo.

Leo has a rare bone condition and suffers severe injury.

Maya is liable for the full injury.

Memory:

You break it, you bought all the damage.

32. Damages in negligence

Negligence requires actual harm.

No nominal damages.

Recoverable damages include:

medical costs
pain and suffering
lost income
reduced earning capacity
property damage
Example

Maya carelessly knocks Leo’s phone into a fountain.

Leo can recover property damage.

If nothing is harmed, no negligence recovery.

Memory:

Negligence needs real harm.

33. Pure economic loss

Usually, pure money loss without physical injury or property damage is not recoverable in ordinary negligence.

Example

Maya’s careless street closure makes Leo’s shop lose customers for one day.

No physical injury. No property damage.

Usually no ordinary negligence claim.

Exception: professionals can sometimes be liable for negligent services causing economic loss.

Memory:

Pure money loss usually needs contract, warranty, or professional misrepresentation.

34. NIED

Negligent infliction of emotional distress has three main paths.

Zone of danger

Plaintiff was nearly physically hit/harmed.

Example

Maya drops a heavy sign. It narrowly misses Leo. Leo suffers serious emotional distress.

Possible NIED.

Bystander

Plaintiff sees close family member injured.

Example

Leo watches his child get hit by falling equipment.

Possible NIED.

Special relationship

No physical danger required.

Example

A funeral home loses a family member’s ashes.

Possible NIED.

Memory:

NIED = danger zone, close-family bystander, or special relationship.

35. Vicarious liability

Vicarious liability means:

One person is liable for another person’s tort because of their relationship.

Most important:

Employer is liable for employee’s torts within scope of employment.

Example

A bakery delivery employee negligently knocks over a pedestrian while delivering pastries.

Bakery may be liable.

Memory:

Employee on the job = employer may pay.

Detour vs frolic

Small work-related side trip = detour = employer may still be liable.

Big personal trip = frolic = employer not liable.

Example

Employee stops for coffee while making deliveries.

Detour.

Employee drives across town to visit a friend.

Frolic.

36. Independent contractors

General rule:

Hiring party is not vicariously liable for independent contractor’s torts.

But exceptions:

retained control
nondelegable duty
apparent agency
negligent hiring/supervision
abnormally dangerous activity
Example

A grocery store hires a contractor to polish floors.

Contractor leaves the entrance dangerously slick.

Customer falls.

Store may still be liable because keeping premises safe for customers is nondelegable.

Memory:

You can delegate the work, not certain duties.

37. Parents and children

Parents are usually not automatically liable for their child’s torts.

But parents can be liable for their own negligence.

Example

Parent knows child keeps throwing bricks from a balcony and does nothing.

Parent may be liable.

Memory:

Parent not automatically liable, but liable for bad supervision/control.

38. Joint and several liability

If multiple defendants cause one indivisible injury, plaintiff may collect the whole judgment from one defendant.

Example

Two careless contractors cause one ceiling collapse.

Plaintiff has one indivisible injury.

Plaintiff may collect full damages from either, unless jurisdiction limits joint and several liability.

Memory:

Joint and several = plaintiff can collect all from one.

39. Contribution vs indemnity
Contribution

One defendant pays more than their fair share and seeks partial reimbursement.

Indemnity

One defendant shifts the whole loss to another.

Example

Employer pays for employee’s negligence.

Employer may seek indemnity from employee.

Memory:

Contribution = split the bill. Indemnity = shift the whole bill.

40. Comparative and contributory negligence
Contributory negligence

Old harsh rule.

Plaintiff is 1% at fault = plaintiff gets nothing.

Pure comparative negligence

Plaintiff always recovers defendant’s percentage.

If plaintiff is 80% at fault and damages are $100,000, plaintiff gets $20,000.

Modified comparative negligence

Plaintiff recovers only if not too at fault.

Memory:

Contributory = one mistake can bar.
Pure comparative = always reduced.
Modified = reduced unless plaintiff too responsible.

41. Assumption of risk

Plaintiff knowingly and voluntarily accepts a specific risk.

Example

Leo sees a gym machine is broken and still uses it for fun.

His recovery may be barred or reduced.

Memory:

Known risk + voluntary choice = assumption of risk.

42. Strict liability

Strict liability means:

Defendant can be liable even if careful.

Only usually for DAD:

Dangerous activities
Animals
Defective products

Memory:

DAD = strict liability buckets.

43. Abnormally dangerous activities

Strict liability applies when activity:

Creates a highly significant risk even with reasonable care, and
Is not common.
Example

A company stores large amounts of toxic gas in a residential neighborhood.

Gas escapes and harms nearby homes.

Strict liability possible.

Memory:

Danger remains even when careful = strict liability.

But the harm must come from the danger that makes the activity dangerous.

If a worker carrying toxic gas slips and drops a clipboard on someone’s foot, that is not the special toxic-gas risk.

44. Animals
Wild animals

Owner is strictly liable for characteristic dangerous harms.

Example

Maya keeps a pet wolf.

The wolf bites a visitor.

Strict liability.

Memory:

Wild stays wild, even if trained.

Domestic animals

Usually negligence unless owner knows animal has abnormal dangerous tendencies.

Example

Maya’s housecat has never attacked anyone.

No strict liability at common law.

But if the cat has repeatedly attacked visitors and Maya knows, strict liability may apply.

Trespassing animals

Owner/possessor of livestock-like animals is strictly liable for foreseeable property damage caused while trespassing.

Memory:

Animal liability follows the animal owner/possessor.

45. Products liability

A product case can be brought under:

Negligence
Strict products liability
Warranty

Products can be defective in three ways:

Manufacturing defect
Design defect
Failure to warn

Memory:

Manufacture = bad one. Design = bad plan. Warning = bad instructions.

46. Manufacturing defect

One item came out wrong.

Example

A factory makes 10,000 heaters.

One heater has a loose internal wire because of an assembly mistake.

Manufacturing defect.

Memory:

Manufacturing defect = this one is different from the rest.

47. Design defect

The whole product line is designed dangerously.

Tests:

Consumer expectation

More dangerous than ordinary consumer expects.

Risk-utility

Risks outweigh benefits, often requiring a reasonable alternative design.

Example

A foldable ladder is designed without locking hinges, and every ladder of that model can collapse.

Design defect.

Memory:

Design defect = bad blueprint.

48. Failure to warn

There is a foreseeable, non-obvious risk that reasonable warnings could reduce.

Example

A cleaning spray creates dangerous fumes when used near heat, but label says nothing.

Failure to warn.

Memory:

Hidden danger needs warning.

Prescription drugs usually warn the doctor, not the patient.

Memory:

Prescription warning goes to learned intermediary.

49. Products liability defendants

Strict products liability applies to commercial sellers/distributors.

Includes:

manufacturer
distributor
retailer
commercial lessor

Not casual sellers.

Example

Maya sells her old blender at a garage sale.

She is not a strict products liability defendant.

Memory:

Business seller yes. Casual seller no.

50. Products damages

Strict products liability usually covers:

personal injury
damage to other property

Not pure economic loss or harm only to the product itself.

Example

A defective toaster burns Leo’s kitchen wall.

Tort recovery possible.

If toaster simply stops working, that is warranty/contract.

Memory:

People/property damage = tort. Product disappointment = warranty.

51. Warranties

Warranty is more contract-like.

Merchantability

Product is fit for ordinary use.

Example

A chair sold by a furniture store should support normal sitting.

Fitness for particular purpose

Seller knows buyer’s special purpose and buyer relies on seller.

Example

Leo tells store clerk:

“I need gloves safe for handling dry ice.”

Clerk recommends gloves that do not protect from cold burns.

Fitness issue.

Express warranty

Seller makes factual promise.

Example

Seller says:

“This refrigerator keeps food cold for 24 hours during a power outage.”

That can be express warranty.

Memory:

Merchantability = normal use. Fitness = special use. Express = factual promise.

52. Defamation

Defamation protects reputation.

Need:

Defamatory statement
Of or concerning plaintiff
Publication to third party
Damages
Falsity/fault rules if public concern or public figure

Memory:

Defamation = false reputation harm told to someone else.

Example

Maya falsely tells Leo’s coworkers:

“Leo stole client money.”

Defamation.

53. Publication

Publication just means:

Communication to one third person who understands it.

It does not mean newspaper.

Example

Maya whispers false statement about Leo to Noah.

Publication.

Memory:

Publication = told to someone besides plaintiff.

54. Public figure/public concern

Public officials and public figures must prove actual malice.

Actual malice means:

Knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard for truth.

It does not mean hatred.

Memory:

Actual malice = knew false or seriously doubted truth.

Private person + public concern usually needs at least negligence.

55. Libel vs slander
Libel

Written/recorded/permanent.

Slander

Spoken/temporary.

Slander usually requires special damages unless slander per se.

Slander per se categories:

Crime
Business/profession
Loathsome disease
Sexual misconduct

Memory:

Crime, career, disease, sex.

56. Defamation defenses

Truth is a complete defense.

Privileges:

Absolute privilege

Judicial proceedings, legislative proceedings, certain government officials, spouses.

Even false/malicious statements may be protected.

Qualified privilege

Employment references, protecting shared interests, public interest.

Lost if abused or made with malice.

Memory:

Absolute privilege is bulletproof. Qualified privilege can be lost.

57. Invasion of privacy

Mnemonic:

I FLAP

Intrusion
False light
Appropriation
Public disclosure of private facts

Privacy generally protects individuals, not corporations.

58. Intrusion

Intentional invasion into private affairs, highly offensive.

No publication needed.

Example

Maya secretly installs a microphone in Leo’s bedroom.

Intrusion.

Memory:

Intrusion = offensive snooping.

59. False light

Publicly portraying someone in a misleading false way that would be highly offensive.

Example

A website uses Leo’s photo next to an article suggesting he supports a hate group, even though he does not.

False light.

Memory:

False light = misleading public impression.

60. Appropriation

Unauthorized use of plaintiff’s identity for defendant’s advantage.

Example

A cereal company uses Maya’s face on its box without permission.

Appropriation.

Memory:

Appropriation = stealing identity value.

61. Public disclosure of private facts

Publicizing true private facts that are highly offensive and not newsworthy.

Example

Maya posts Leo’s private medical records online.

Public disclosure.

Truth is not a defense here because the issue is privacy.

Memory:

Defamation hates falsehood. Public disclosure hates private truth.

62. Intentional misrepresentation

Also called fraud.

Need:

False material representation
Knowledge/reckless disregard
Intent to induce reliance
Actual reliance
Justifiable reliance
Damages
Example

Maya sells Leo a building and says:

“The roof is brand new.”

She knows it is badly damaged.

Leo buys because of that.

Fraud.

Memory:

Fraud = knowing lie + reliance + loss.

Puffery is not fraud

“This is the nicest building in town” = puffery.

“The roof was replaced in 2023” = fact.

63. Negligent misrepresentation

This is careless false business information.

Need:

defendant in business/profession/pecuniary transaction
false information
lack of reasonable care
reasonable reliance
economic loss

Liability is limited to the person/group and transaction the information was meant to guide.

Example

Maya prepares financial numbers for Leo knowing they will be used for a small supplier credit decision.

A bank later uses them for a huge acquisition.

Maya may not be liable for that different transaction.

Memory:

Negligent misrep = right person, right purpose, right transaction.

64. Intentional interference with contract

Need:

Valid contract between plaintiff and third party
Defendant knew of it
Defendant intentionally interfered
Caused breach
Damages
Example

Maya knows Leo has a fixed contract with a venue.

Maya pays the venue to break the contract and book her instead.

Interference.

Memory:

Known contract + intentional breach-causing = interference.

Fair competition can be a defense, especially for at-will/prospective relationships.

65. Interference with prospective economic advantage

No existing contract needed.

Need intentional improper interference with likely business.

Usually plaintiff must show wrongful conduct.

Example

Maya lies to a potential customer that Leo’s bakery failed health inspection.

Customer cancels planned order.

Possible interference with prospective advantage.

Memory:

No contract yet = prospective advantage, harder claim.

66. Trade secrets

A trade secret is valuable business information not generally known, protected by reasonable precautions, and taken by improper means.

Example

Leo has a secret sauce formula locked in a restricted digital file.

Maya hacks it.

Trade secret theft.

Memory:

Secret + value + protection + improper taking.

67. Trade libel

False statement about plaintiff’s product/business quality causing special damages.

Example

Maya falsely posts:

“Leo’s protein bars contain insects.”

Leo loses store contracts.

Trade libel.

Memory:

Trade libel = false attack on product/business.

68. Slander of title

False statement attacking ownership/title to property, causing special damages.

Example

Maya falsely records a document saying she owns Leo’s warehouse.

Buyer backs out.

Slander of title.

Memory:

Slander of title = false attack on ownership.

69. Malicious prosecution

Wrongfully starting a legal case without probable cause, for improper purpose, and it ends in plaintiff’s favor.

Example

Maya files a baseless criminal complaint against Leo to punish him.

Case is dismissed.

Leo may sue for malicious prosecution.

Memory:

Malicious prosecution = bad case, no probable cause, favorable ending.

70. Abuse of process

Using legal process for an improper purpose after it has begun.

Example

Maya files a normal lawsuit but uses subpoenas only to pressure Leo into selling her his business.

Abuse of process.

Memory:

Malicious prosecution = wrongfully start case.
Abuse of process = misuse legal tools.

Final MBE attack plan

When you read a torts question, ask:

Step 1: What type of harm?
Body/contact? Battery, assault, negligence.
Trapped? False imprisonment.
Emotional harm? IIED or NIED.
Land/property? Trespass, nuisance, conversion.
Carelessness? Negligence.
Dangerous activity/animal/product? Strict liability.
Reputation? Defamation.
Privacy? I FLAP.
Business lie? Misrepresentation.
Contract/business interference? Business tort.
Bad lawsuit/legal pressure? Malicious prosecution or abuse of process.
Step 2: What mental state?
Intended act/contact? Intentional tort.
Careless? Negligence.
No fault needed? Strict liability.
Step 3: What defense?
Consent
Self-defense
Defense of others/property
Necessity
Comparative/contributory negligence
Assumption of risk
Privilege
Truth
Immunity
Step 4: What damages?
Intentional torts often allow nominal damages.
Negligence requires actual harm.
Products/torts usually require personal injury or property damage.
Warranty can cover economic loss.
Defamation/privacy/business torts have special damage rules.
Best memory lines

Battery = intentional contact. Assault = seeing contact coming.

False imprisonment = trapped. IIED = outrageous emotional harm.

Trespass = physical invasion. Nuisance = ruined enjoyment.

Negligence = duty, breach, causation, damages.

Res ipsa = accident suggests negligence.

Strict liability = DAD: dangerous activities, animals, defective products.

Products: manufacturing = bad one; design = bad plan; warning = bad instructions.

Defamation = reputation. Privacy = personal dignity/control.

Fraud = knowing lie. Negligent misrep = careless business info.

Interference with contract = known contract + improper interference.

Malicious prosecution starts a bad case. Abuse of process misuses a case.
