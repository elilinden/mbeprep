## Civil Procedure

### orig-civpro-014 — Supplemental jurisdiction - claim by plaintiff against a Rule 14 party

A citizen of State A sued a trucking company incorporated and having its principal place of business in State B in federal district court. The complaint sought $200,000 for injuries suffered in a highway collision and invoked only diversity jurisdiction. The trucking company filed a proper third-party complaint under Rule 14 against the truck driver, a citizen of State A, seeking indemnity. The plaintiff then asserted a $50,000 negligence claim against the driver arising from the same collision. No federal claim is involved. The driver moved to dismiss the plaintiff's claim for lack of subject-matter jurisdiction.

**Should the court grant the driver's motion?**

**(A)** Yes, because the supplemental-jurisdiction statute bars this claim by the plaintiff against a party joined under Rule 14.
**(B)** Yes, because no claim seeking $75,000 or less may ever fall within supplemental jurisdiction.
**(C)** No, because the plaintiff's claim and the indemnity claim arise from the same collision.
**(D)** No, because the trucking company created jurisdiction over the driver by filing the third-party complaint.

**Key:** A

**Rule:** In an action founded solely on diversity jurisdiction, 28 U.S.C. § 1367(b) bars supplemental jurisdiction over claims by plaintiffs against persons made parties under Rule 14 when exercising jurisdiction would be inconsistent with 28 U.S.C. § 1332.

**Application:** The driver was joined under Rule 14, is a citizen of the same state as the plaintiff, and faces a claim for only $50,000. The plaintiff's claim therefore lacks an independent diversity basis and falls within § 1367(b).

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** When original jurisdiction rests solely on diversity, 28 U.S.C. § 1367(b) withholds supplemental jurisdiction over a claim by the original plaintiff against a person made a party under Rule 14 if the claim does not independently satisfy diversity requirements.
- **B:** Related claims below the diversity amount can ordinarily fall within supplemental jurisdiction. The problem here is the specific limitation in § 1367(b), not a universal amount-in-controversy rule.
- **C:** The claims form part of the same Article III case or controversy, but § 1367(b) expressly removes supplemental jurisdiction over this type of claim by a plaintiff in a diversity-only action.
- **D:** The court may have supplemental jurisdiction over the defendant's indemnity claim, but that does not supply an independent jurisdictional basis for the original plaintiff's claim against a nondiverse third-party defendant.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass clarified that the original action rests solely on diversity, specified the third-party defendant's citizenship and amount, and removed any possible federal-question basis.

**Authority notes:** 28 U.S.C. §§ 1332 and 1367(a)-(b); Fed. R. Civ. P. 14(a)(3)

### orig-civpro-015 — Rule 12 defenses - waiver by omission from the first Rule 12 motion

A plaintiff properly filed a federal action against a defendant. Before answering, the defendant moved under Rule 12 to dismiss for failure to state a claim. The motion did not raise lack of personal jurisdiction. After the court denied that motion, but before the defendant filed an answer, the defendant filed a second Rule 12 motion seeking dismissal for lack of personal jurisdiction. The plaintiff opposed the second motion on the ground that the defense had been waived.

**How should the court rule on the second motion?**

**(A)** Grant it, because a personal-jurisdiction defense may be raised at any time before trial.
**(B)** Deny it, because the defendant waived the defense by omitting it from the first Rule 12 motion.
**(C)** Grant it, because the defendant had not yet filed a responsive pleading when the second motion was made.
**(D)** Deny it only if the defendant had actual notice of the personal-jurisdiction defect when filing the first motion.

**Key:** B

**Rule:** A party that makes a Rule 12 motion must consolidate available defenses under Rule 12(b)(2)-(5). A defense of lack of personal jurisdiction is waived if omitted from that motion.

**Application:** The defendant first moved under Rule 12(b)(6) without asserting lack of personal jurisdiction. The later Rule 12(b)(2) motion is therefore barred by the consolidation and waiver rules.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** Unlike subject-matter jurisdiction, personal jurisdiction is a waivable defense and must be included in the first Rule 12 response when Rule 12(g) requires consolidation.
- **B:** Rule 12(g)(2) and Rule 12(h)(1) require a defendant to consolidate the defenses listed in Rule 12(b)(2)-(5) in the first Rule 12 motion. Omission waives the personal-jurisdiction defense.
- **C:** The filing of an answer is not the relevant event. The defendant already made a Rule 12 motion and was required to include the personal-jurisdiction defense in it.
- **D:** Waiver under Rules 12(g) and 12(h) does not depend on proof that the defendant subjectively recognized the omitted defense.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass made clear that the first motion preceded the answer and that the omitted defense was personal jurisdiction, one of the waivable Rule 12(b)(2)-(5) defenses.

**Authority notes:** Fed. R. Civ. P. 12(g)(2), 12(h)(1)

### orig-civpro-016 — Preliminary injunctions and temporary restraining orders - notice requirement

A homeowner filed a federal action challenging a city's planned demolition of her house. The city announced that demolition would begin the next morning. The homeowner submitted a verified complaint describing immediate and irreparable harm and asked the court to issue a preliminary injunction without notifying the city. Her attorney filed a written certification describing unsuccessful efforts to reach the city attorney and explaining why notice could not be completed before demolition.

**May the court issue the requested preliminary injunction without notice to the city?**

**(A)** Yes, because the verified complaint establishes a threat of immediate and irreparable injury.
**(B)** Yes, because the attorney documented unsuccessful efforts to provide notice.
**(C)** No, because a preliminary injunction may issue only after notice, although an ex parte temporary restraining order may be available.
**(D)** No, because federal courts may never grant emergency injunctive relief before the adverse party appears.

**Key:** C

**Rule:** A preliminary injunction may issue only on notice to the adverse party. A court may issue a temporary restraining order without notice only if specific facts show immediate and irreparable injury and counsel certifies efforts to give notice and reasons notice should not be required.

**Application:** The homeowner has facts potentially supporting an ex parte TRO, but the court cannot issue the requested preliminary injunction until the city receives notice.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** Immediate injury may support an ex parte temporary restraining order if Rule 65(b) is satisfied, but a preliminary injunction may issue only on notice to the adverse party.
- **B:** The certification is relevant to an ex parte TRO. It does not eliminate Rule 65(a)(1)'s notice requirement for a preliminary injunction.
- **C:** Rule 65(a)(1) categorically requires notice before a preliminary injunction. The facts may satisfy the separate requirements for an ex parte TRO.
- **D:** Rule 65(b) permits a narrowly limited ex parte TRO when the specified factual showing and attorney certification are made.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass separated the relief requested from relief potentially available and included both Rule 65(b) prerequisites so the question does not turn on a missing TRO fact.

**Authority notes:** Fed. R. Civ. P. 65(a)(1), 65(b)(1)

### orig-civpro-017 — Discovery - physical examination under Rule 35

In a federal personal-injury action, the plaintiff alleged that a collision caused a permanent spinal injury. The defendant served a notice directing the plaintiff to appear for an examination by an orthopedic physician selected by the defendant. The notice specified the time, place, manner, conditions, and scope of the examination. The defendant did not move for a court order. The plaintiff refused to attend, and the defendant moved to compel compliance based solely on the notice.

**Should the court compel the plaintiff to attend the examination?**

**(A)** Yes, because the plaintiff placed her physical condition in controversy by seeking damages for a permanent injury.
**(B)** Yes, because a party may obtain any nonprivileged discovery proportional to the needs of the case by serving notice.
**(C)** No, because a party may be examined only by a physician selected jointly by the parties.
**(D)** No, because a physical examination requires a court order entered on motion for good cause.

**Key:** D

**Rule:** A court may order a party whose physical or mental condition is in controversy to submit to an examination only on motion for good cause and notice to the person and all parties. The order must specify the examination's details.

**Application:** Although the plaintiff's spinal condition is in controversy and the notice contains detailed terms, the defendant never obtained the required court order.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** Putting the condition in controversy is necessary but not sufficient. Rule 35 requires a court order made on motion for good cause and with notice to the person and all parties.
- **B:** The general scope of discovery does not displace Rule 35's specific requirement of a court order for a physical or mental examination.
- **C:** Rule 35 does not require joint selection. The court order may specify a suitably licensed or certified examiner.
- **D:** A Rule 35 examination is not self-executing through notice alone. The defendant must obtain an order after showing that the condition is in controversy and good cause exists.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass removed any dispute about whether the condition was in controversy and made the missing court order the single dispositive defect.

**Authority notes:** Fed. R. Civ. P. 35(a)

### orig-civpro-018 — Final judgment rule - unresolved claims and Rule 54(b)

A plaintiff asserted separate breach-of-contract and fraud claims against a defendant in federal district court. The court granted summary judgment to the defendant on the contract claim but left the fraud claim for trial. The court did not direct entry of judgment on the contract claim and did not determine that there was no just reason for delay. The plaintiff immediately filed a notice of appeal from the ruling on the contract claim.

**Should the court of appeals hear the appeal?**

**(A)** No, because the order did not resolve all claims and the district court made no Rule 54(b) certification.
**(B)** No, because an order granting summary judgment can never be appealed before trial on the remaining claims.
**(C)** Yes, because summary judgment conclusively resolved the contract claim.
**(D)** Yes, because the contract and fraud claims are legally distinct causes of action.

**Key:** A

**Rule:** Under the final-judgment rule, an appeal ordinarily lies only after all claims as to all parties are resolved. In a multiclaim action, the district court may direct entry of final judgment on fewer than all claims only by expressly determining that there is no just reason for delay.

**Application:** The fraud claim remains pending, and the district court made neither of the findings required by Rule 54(b). The summary-judgment order is therefore not yet final.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** An order disposing of fewer than all claims ordinarily is not final. Without an express direction for entry of judgment and a determination that there is no just reason for delay, the ruling remains interlocutory.
- **B:** A partial summary judgment may be immediately appealable if properly certified under Rule 54(b), and other exceptions may also permit interlocutory review.
- **C:** Conclusive resolution of one claim is not enough when another claim remains and there is no Rule 54(b) certification.
- **D:** The distinctness of the claims permits potential Rule 54(b) certification but does not itself create a final, appealable judgment.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass specified that the claims are separate but that no Rule 54(b) direction or no-just-reason finding was made.

**Authority notes:** 28 U.S.C. § 1291; Fed. R. Civ. P. 54(b)

## Constitutional Law

### orig-conlaw-014 — State sovereign immunity - prospective relief against a state officer

A state statute required the director of a state licensing agency to revoke the license of any business that published criticism of the governor. A newspaper company whose license was about to be revoked filed an action in federal district court against the director in her official capacity. The complaint alleged that the statute violated the First Amendment and sought only an injunction preventing the director from enforcing the statute in the future. The state had not consented to suit.

**Is the action barred by state sovereign immunity?**

**(A)** Yes, because a suit against a state officer in her official capacity is always treated as a suit against the state.
**(B)** No, because the company seeks prospective relief against a state officer for an alleged ongoing violation of federal law.
**(C)** Yes, because Congress has not abrogated state immunity for this particular action.
**(D)** No, but only because the First Amendment automatically eliminates sovereign immunity for every form of relief.

**Key:** B

**Rule:** State sovereign immunity does not bar an action against a state officer in her official capacity for prospective injunctive relief designed to end an ongoing violation of federal law.

**Application:** The company seeks no damages or payment from the state treasury. It asks only that the director be enjoined from future enforcement of the challenged statute.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** Official-capacity suits for retrospective damages generally implicate state immunity, but Ex parte Young permits suits against state officers for prospective relief from ongoing violations of federal law.
- **B:** The Ex parte Young doctrine allows a federal court to enjoin a state officer from prospectively enforcing an allegedly unconstitutional state law.
- **C:** Congressional abrogation is not required when the plaintiff invokes the Ex parte Young exception and seeks prospective relief against a responsible state officer.
- **D:** The type of relief matters. The doctrine permits prospective injunctive relief; it does not automatically authorize retrospective monetary relief against the state.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass limited the requested remedy to prospective injunctive relief and identified the officer charged with enforcement, avoiding ambiguity about retrospective treasury relief.

**Authority notes:** Ex parte Young, 209 U.S. 123 (1908); Edelman v. Jordan, 415 U.S. 651 (1974)

### orig-conlaw-015 — Congressional commerce power - aggregation of intrastate economic activity

Congress enacted a comprehensive statute regulating the national market for a fungible grain that is bought and sold throughout the United States. To stabilize supply and prices, the statute limited the amount of the grain that each farm could produce. A farmer knowingly exceeded the limit but used all of the excess grain to feed animals on his own farm and neither sold nor transported it. The federal government imposed a civil penalty on the farmer under the statute.

**Is the penalty constitutional?**

**(A)** No, because Congress may regulate only goods that actually cross a state line.
**(B)** No, because production for personal consumption is noneconomic activity beyond the commerce power.
**(C)** Yes, because Congress may regulate the farmer's production as part of a comprehensive regulation of an interstate market.
**(D)** Yes, but only if the government proves that this farmer's excess grain alone changed interstate prices.

**Key:** C

**Rule:** Congress may regulate intrastate economic activity that, in the aggregate, substantially affects interstate commerce, particularly when regulation is an essential part of a broader scheme governing an interstate market.

**Application:** The grain is a fungible market commodity. Production for on-farm use substitutes for purchases from the national market, and aggregate excess production could undermine Congress's supply controls.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** Congress may regulate intrastate economic activity when the activity, considered in the aggregate, has a substantial effect on interstate commerce.
- **B:** Producing a market commodity is economic activity. Home consumption can affect the interstate market by reducing demand for grain purchased in commerce.
- **C:** The farmer's production is economic and, when aggregated with similar production, could substantially affect supply and demand in the interstate grain market.
- **D:** Congress may consider the cumulative effect of the regulated class of economic activity; it need not prove a substantial interstate effect from one farmer's conduct in isolation.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass described a comprehensive regulation of a fungible interstate commodity and avoided relying on noneconomic conduct, which would trigger a different Commerce Clause analysis.

**Authority notes:** Wickard v. Filburn, 317 U.S. 111 (1942); Gonzales v. Raich, 545 U.S. 1 (2005)

### orig-conlaw-016 — Presidential power - refusal to spend funds that Congress directed be disbursed

Congress enacted a statute directing the Secretary of Transportation to distribute a specified sum to every city that met stated eligibility criteria. The statute appropriated the money and gave the executive branch no discretion to reduce an eligible city's award. All eligible cities were identified. The President, believing that immediate distribution would worsen inflation, ordered the Secretary to withhold half of the appropriated funds. No other statute authorized the withholding.

**Is the President's order constitutional?**

**(A)** Yes, because the President has inherent authority to control the timing and amount of all federal expenditures.
**(B)** Yes, because inflation control is an area in which presidential policy judgments are not judicially reviewable.
**(C)** No, because the President may reduce an appropriation only by exercising a line-item veto.
**(D)** No, because the President may not disregard Congress's mandatory direction to distribute the appropriated funds.

**Key:** D

**Rule:** The President must faithfully execute valid federal statutes and may not frustrate a clear congressional command to spend appropriated funds when Congress has provided no discretion to withhold them.

**Application:** The statute identifies the recipients and amounts and directs distribution. The President's inflation concern does not authorize withholding contrary to the statutory command.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** The President must execute duly enacted spending laws and has no general power to cancel or reduce mandatory appropriations based solely on policy disagreement.
- **B:** The issue is whether the President complied with a statutory command, a question courts can adjudicate. A policy justification does not create discretion the statute withheld.
- **C:** The Constitution does not grant the President a line-item veto, and the Supreme Court has held a statutory line-item veto unconstitutional. That is not the source of the defect here.
- **D:** When Congress has lawfully required disbursement and left no executive discretion, the President must faithfully execute that command rather than substitute a contrary spending policy.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass eliminated any statutory discretion and framed the measure as a completed appropriation to identified eligible recipients, making the Take Care issue dispositive.

**Authority notes:** U.S. Const. art. II, § 3 (Take Care Clause); Train v. City of New York, 420 U.S. 35 (1975); Clinton v. City of New York, 524 U.S. 417 (1998)

### orig-conlaw-017 — Equal protection - state alienage classification and political-function exception

A state operated several public hospitals. A state statute limited all permanent nursing positions at those hospitals to United States citizens. A lawful permanent resident who had lived in the state for 12 years, held every required professional license, and was otherwise qualified was denied a nursing position solely because she was not a citizen. She challenged the statute under the Equal Protection Clause.

**Which standard should a court apply to the citizenship restriction?**

**(A)** Strict scrutiny, because nursing is not a position intimately related to democratic self-government.
**(B)** Intermediate scrutiny, because citizenship classifications are treated in the same manner as sex classifications.
**(C)** Rational basis review, because every employee of a state institution performs a governmental function.
**(D)** Rational basis review, because lawful permanent residents are not protected by the Equal Protection Clause.

**Key:** A

**Rule:** State alienage classifications generally are subject to strict scrutiny, except for positions intimately related to the process of democratic self-government, for which rational basis review applies.

**Application:** Hospital nurses provide professional medical care but do not formulate or execute sovereign policy. The narrow political-function exception therefore does not apply.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** State alienage classifications ordinarily receive strict scrutiny. The political-function exception is narrow and does not cover ordinary professional nursing duties in a public hospital.
- **B:** State classifications based on lawful alienage generally trigger strict scrutiny, not intermediate scrutiny.
- **C:** The political-function exception does not encompass every public employee. It covers positions involving discretionary participation in the formulation or execution of public policy.
- **D:** Lawful permanent residents are persons protected by the Equal Protection Clause, and state alienage classifications generally receive heightened review.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass selected a licensed clinical position with no policy-making authority, avoiding uncertainty about jobs such as police officers, teachers, or notaries that have generated political-function litigation.

**Authority notes:** Graham v. Richardson, 403 U.S. 365 (1971); Sugarman v. Dougall, 413 U.S. 634 (1973); Bernal v. Fainter, 467 U.S. 216 (1984)

### orig-conlaw-018 — Freedom of expression - licensing scheme with unbridled discretion

A city ordinance required a person to obtain a permit before distributing leaflets in any city park. The parks director could deny a permit whenever, in the director's judgment, the proposed distribution would be “inconsistent with the public welfare.” The ordinance supplied no additional standards, imposed no deadline for a decision, and provided no prompt judicial review. An advocacy group that wished to distribute political leaflets brought a facial challenge before applying for a permit.

**Is the ordinance constitutional?**

**(A)** Yes, because the group has not yet applied for and been denied a permit.
**(B)** No, because the ordinance gives the director unbounded discretion and lacks adequate procedural safeguards.
**(C)** Yes, because a city may regulate all expressive activity occurring on government-owned property.
**(D)** No, but only because political leaflets may never be subject to a permit requirement.

**Key:** B

**Rule:** A licensing scheme affecting protected expression must employ narrow, objective, definite standards and adequate procedural safeguards. A person may facially challenge a scheme granting unbridled discretion without first seeking a license.

**Application:** The director may deny a permit based on an undefined public-welfare judgment, with no decision deadline or prompt review. Those features invite censorship and indefinite delay.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** A speaker may bring a facial challenge to a licensing scheme that vests unbridled discretion in an official without first applying for a permit.
- **B:** A prior-licensing system for protected speech must contain narrow, objective standards and adequate procedures. The public-welfare standard, absence of deadlines, and lack of prompt review create a classic prior-restraint defect.
- **C:** Traditional public forums such as parks receive strong First Amendment protection. Government ownership does not permit unrestricted licensing discretion.
- **D:** A content-neutral permit scheme may be valid if it uses objective standards, serves legitimate interests, and includes proper procedural safeguards. The defect is not the mere existence of a permit.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass added the lack of deadlines and prompt review while preserving unbridled discretion as the central defect; it also made the facial-challenge posture explicit.

**Authority notes:** Shuttlesworth v. City of Birmingham, 394 U.S. 147 (1969); City of Lakewood v. Plain Dealer Publishing Co., 486 U.S. 750 (1988)

### orig-contracts-014 — UCC acceptance by shipment - nonconforming goods sent as an accommodation

A furniture retailer sent a signed purchase order to a wholesaler requesting shipment of 100 blue chairs at $50 each, delivery by June 1. The wholesaler promptly shipped 100 red chairs. With the shipment, the wholesaler sent a conspicuous notice stating, “Blue chairs are unavailable. These red chairs are offered only as an accommodation.” The retailer rejected the shipment and sued the wholesaler for breach of contract.

**Is the wholesaler liable for breach?**

**(A)** Yes, because shipment of nonconforming goods simultaneously accepts the order and breaches the resulting contract.
**(B)** Yes, because a seller may make a counteroffer only before shipping any goods.
**(C)** No, because the seasonably notified accommodation shipment was a counteroffer rather than an acceptance.
**(D)** No, because a contract for goods is not formed until the buyer accepts delivery.

**Key:** C

**Rule:** An order inviting prompt shipment may be accepted by shipping conforming or nonconforming goods. A shipment of nonconforming goods ordinarily is both acceptance and breach, unless the seller seasonably notifies the buyer that the shipment is offered only as an accommodation, in which case it is a counteroffer.

**Application:** The wholesaler's notice accompanied the red chairs and expressly characterized them as an accommodation. The retailer rejected the counteroffer, so the shipment did not create a contract that the wholesaler breached.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** That is the usual rule when a seller ships nonconforming goods without seasonably identifying the shipment as an accommodation. The accompanying accommodation notice changes the result.
- **B:** Under UCC Article 2, a seasonably notified accommodation shipment operates as a counteroffer even though the goods are shipped.
- **C:** The purchase order invited acceptance by prompt shipment, but the wholesaler clearly and seasonably stated that the nonconforming shipment was offered only as an accommodation. No contract arose unless the retailer accepted that counteroffer.
- **D:** A sales contract can be formed before delivery through the parties' communications or by shipment. Buyer acceptance of delivered goods is not always required for formation.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass placed the accommodation notice with the shipment, made it conspicuous and explicit, and avoided facts suggesting a prior contract independent of the purchase order.

**Authority notes:** U.C.C. § 2-206(1)(b)

### orig-contracts-015 — Statute of frauds - possibility of performance within one year

An engineering firm orally promised to pay a consultant $12,000 per month to supervise construction of a tunnel until the tunnel was completed. Both parties expected the project to take approximately 18 months, but the agreement did not establish a minimum duration, and completion within 10 months was physically possible. After three months, the firm discharged the consultant without cause and refused to pay amounts then due.

**Is the agreement unenforceable under the one-year provision of the Statute of Frauds?**

**(A)** Yes, because both parties expected performance to continue for longer than one year.
**(B)** Yes, because the consultant was to receive payments extending beyond one year if construction proceeded as expected.
**(C)** No, but only because the firm partially performed by paying the consultant for three months.
**(D)** No, because the tunnel could have been completed within one year of making the agreement.

**Key:** D

**Rule:** The one-year provision applies only if, by its terms, the contract cannot possibly be fully performed within one year from formation. Expected or probable duration does not control.

**Application:** The parties expected an 18-month project, but the agreement ended upon completion and the tunnel could have been completed within 10 months. Full performance within one year was therefore possible.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** The one-year provision turns on whether complete performance is possible within one year, not on the parties' expectations or probabilities.
- **B:** The expected payment schedule is not controlling when the contracted task could be fully completed within one year.
- **C:** Partial performance generally does not remove a service contract from the one-year provision. The agreement is outside that provision because full performance was possible within one year.
- **D:** A contract is within the one-year provision only when its terms make performance within one year impossible. The possibility of completing the tunnel within 10 months keeps this agreement outside the provision.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass stated that completion within 10 months was physically possible and that the contract imposed no minimum term, removing uncertainty about theoretical versus contractual possibility.

**Authority notes:** Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 130

### orig-contracts-016 — Assignment of rights - effect of a prohibition that does not make assignments void

An architect completed all required services under a contract with a building owner and became entitled to a final payment of $60,000. The contract stated, “The architect shall not assign any right under this agreement.” It did not state that an assignment would be void or invalid. The architect nevertheless assigned the right to the final payment to a bank as security for a loan. The bank immediately notified the owner. The owner later paid the architect instead of the bank.

**May the bank recover the $60,000 from the owner?**

**(A)** Yes, because the assignment was effective even though making it may have breached the anti-assignment promise.
**(B)** Yes, but only because the architect had fully performed before making the assignment.
**(C)** No, because any assignment made in violation of an anti-assignment clause is automatically ineffective.
**(D)** No, because the owner discharged the payment obligation by paying the architect, the party named in the contract.

**Key:** A

**Rule:** A term prohibiting assignment ordinarily creates a contractual duty not to assign but does not make an assignment ineffective unless a contrary intention is clearly manifested. Once the obligor receives notice of an effective assignment, payment to the assignor generally does not discharge the obligation.

**Application:** The architect assigned an accrued right to money, the clause did not declare assignments void, and the owner had notice before paying the architect. The bank may enforce the payment right against the owner.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** A contractual prohibition ordinarily gives the obligor a claim for breach but does not invalidate an assignment unless the contract manifests an intent that an assignment be void. After notice, payment to the assignor does not discharge the owner's duty to the assignee.
- **B:** Full performance reduces concerns about materially changing the obligor's burden, but the central reason the assignment is effective is that the clause did not make assignments void. The same interpretive rule can apply before full performance, subject to other limits.
- **C:** A mere promise not to assign ordinarily does not destroy the power to assign. Clear language declaring assignments void or invalid is generally required for that result.
- **D:** Once the owner received notice of the effective assignment, payment to the architect was at the owner's risk and did not discharge the obligation owed to the bank.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass limited the assignment to an accrued monetary right, specified that the clause did not use voiding language, and established notice before payment.

**Authority notes:** Restatement (Second) of Contracts §§ 317, 322

### orig-contracts-017 — Liquidated damages - reasonable forecast of difficult-to-measure loss

A concert promoter hired a contractor to renovate an arena by May 1. At contracting, the parties knew that delay could require relocating events and losing uncertain ticket and concession revenue. They agreed that the contractor would pay $20,000 for each day of delay. Based on the information then available, both parties reasonably estimated likely delay losses at approximately $20,000 per day. The contractor finished five days late. The promoter's provable losses for those five days were $80,000.

**Is the promoter entitled to enforce the $100,000 liquidated-damages provision?**

**(A)** No, because liquidated damages are unenforceable whenever they exceed the loss ultimately proved at trial.
**(B)** Yes, because the amount was a reasonable estimate of losses that were difficult to measure when the contract was made.
**(C)** No, because a daily charge for delay is necessarily an unenforceable penalty.
**(D)** Yes, because parties may enforce any damages amount to which they expressly agree in writing.

**Key:** B

**Rule:** A liquidated-damages clause is enforceable when actual loss is difficult to estimate and the stipulated amount is reasonable in light of anticipated or actual harm. A grossly disproportionate amount designed to compel performance is a penalty.

**Application:** Delay losses were uncertain when the contract was formed, the parties reasonably estimated $20,000 per day, and the stipulated $100,000 is close to the $80,000 loss ultimately proved.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** A reasonable liquidated-damages amount is not invalid merely because actual loss later differs. Reasonableness is assessed in light of anticipated or actual loss and the difficulty of proof.
- **B:** The clause reasonably forecasted uncertain delay damages, and the $100,000 total is not grossly disproportionate to the $80,000 loss ultimately proved.
- **C:** Per-day delay provisions are commonly enforceable when they reasonably estimate difficult-to-measure losses rather than punish breach.
- **D:** Freedom of contract does not permit enforcement of a penalty. The clause is enforceable because it is reasonable, not merely because it was written and agreed upon.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass narrowed the gap between stipulated and actual loss and expressly established the reasonableness of the contemporaneous estimate, reducing penalty ambiguity.

**Authority notes:** Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 356; U.C.C. § 2-718(1) (analogous sales rule)

### orig-contracts-018 — Third-party beneficiaries - vesting and modification

An uncle promised a contractor that, in exchange for remodeling the uncle's office, the uncle would pay $30,000 directly to the contractor's daughter when she graduated from college. The contract expressly identified the daughter and the payment. The daughter did not know of the contract. Before she learned of it, relied on it, assented to it, or brought suit, the uncle and the contractor mutually agreed to rescind the payment provision. The daughter later graduated and learned of the original agreement.

**May the daughter enforce the original payment provision?**

**(A)** Yes, because an intended beneficiary's rights vest immediately when the contract is formed.
**(B)** Yes, because the daughter satisfied the stated condition by graduating from college.
**(C)** No, because the contracting parties rescinded the provision before the daughter's rights vested.
**(D)** No, because a person who furnishes no consideration can never enforce a contract made for that person's benefit.

**Key:** C

**Rule:** Unless the contract provides otherwise, the promisor and promisee may modify or discharge an intended beneficiary's right until the beneficiary materially changes position in justifiable reliance, brings suit, or manifests assent at a party's request.

**Application:** The daughter was specifically intended to receive payment, but she had no knowledge and took none of the actions that would vest her rights before the rescission.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** An intended beneficiary may have enforceable rights, but the original parties generally retain power to modify or rescind until those rights vest through assent, suit, or material reliance.
- **B:** Graduation would have triggered payment under the original term, but the term was validly rescinded before the daughter's beneficiary rights vested.
- **C:** The daughter was an intended beneficiary, but she had neither assented, sued, nor materially relied before the parties rescinded. They therefore retained the power to eliminate the benefit.
- **D:** An intended third-party beneficiary need not furnish consideration. The daughter loses because her rights had not vested before rescission, not because she gave no consideration.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass expressly negated each recognized vesting event and placed rescission before the daughter learned of the agreement.

**Authority notes:** Restatement (Second) of Contracts §§ 304, 311

## Criminal Law and Procedure

### orig-crim-014 — Embezzlement - conversion of property lawfully possessed

An investor delivered $50,000 to a financial manager under an agreement requiring the manager to hold and invest the money in securities for the investor. The manager lawfully took possession and placed the money in a segregated client account. Several weeks later, the manager decided to use the money to pay personal gambling debts, withdrew it, and permanently converted it to his own use. The jurisdiction defines property crimes according to the common law.

**Of which crime is the manager most clearly guilty?**

**(A)** Larceny, because he intended to deprive the investor permanently of the money.
**(B)** Larceny by trick, because the investor transferred possession but not title to the money.
**(C)** False pretenses, because he obtained the money through a position of trust.
**(D)** Embezzlement, because he fraudulently converted property that was lawfully in his possession.

**Key:** D

**Rule:** Embezzlement is the fraudulent conversion of property of another by a person who is in lawful possession of that property. Larceny requires a trespassory taking, and false pretenses requires obtaining title by fraud.

**Application:** The manager lawfully possessed the money under the investment agreement and formed the intent to appropriate it only weeks later. His later conversion is embezzlement.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** Larceny requires a trespassory taking from another's possession. The manager obtained possession lawfully under the investment agreement before forming the intent to convert the money.
- **B:** Larceny by trick requires fraud at the time possession is obtained. The manager lawfully received the money and formed the fraudulent intent only afterward.
- **C:** False pretenses requires obtaining title through an intentional material misrepresentation. The investor did not transfer title to the manager, and no false representation induced the transfer.
- **D:** The manager had lawful possession of the investor's money under the investment agreement and later intentionally converted it. Those facts satisfy common-law embezzlement.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass used an independent financial manager with a segregated client account, fixed the intent after lawful possession, and stated that the jurisdiction uses common-law offense definitions.

**Authority notes:** Common-law embezzlement doctrine

### orig-crim-015 — Self-defense - initial aggressor who withdraws and communicates withdrawal

During an argument, a man deliberately shoved another person and raised his fists, intending to start a fistfight. The other person pulled a knife. The man immediately stepped backward, lowered his hands, and said, “I am done. I do not want to fight.” The other person advanced with the knife and threatened to kill him. Unable to retreat safely, the man reasonably believed that he was about to be stabbed and used deadly force to stop the attack. The jurisdiction follows the common-law rules of self-defense.

**May the man successfully claim self-defense?**

**(A)** Yes, because he clearly withdrew from the encounter and communicated that withdrawal before using deadly force.
**(B)** Yes, because a person who responds with a knife becomes the sole aggressor regardless of how the encounter began.
**(C)** No, because an initial aggressor can never regain the right to use self-defense during the same encounter.
**(D)** No, because deadly force is never permissible in response to a knife attack that has not yet caused injury.

**Key:** A

**Rule:** An initial aggressor ordinarily cannot claim self-defense, but may regain the privilege by withdrawing in good faith and effectively communicating the withdrawal. Deadly force is then justified if reasonably necessary to prevent imminent death or serious bodily harm.

**Application:** The man stepped back, disarmed his posture, expressly ended the confrontation, and faced an advancing knife attack with no safe retreat. His withdrawal restored the defense before he used deadly force.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** An initial aggressor can regain the right of self-defense by actually withdrawing and communicating the withdrawal. The other person then continued with a threatened deadly attack.
- **B:** Escalation can affect the aggressor analysis, but it does not automatically erase the first actor's aggressor status in every case. The man's clear withdrawal and communication are what restore his privilege here.
- **C:** An initial aggressor may regain the defense by withdrawing in good faith and effectively communicating that withdrawal.
- **D:** A person need not wait to be injured. Deadly force may be used when the person reasonably believes it is necessary to prevent imminent death or serious bodily harm.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass added an explicit verbal and physical withdrawal, an imminent deadly threat, and no safe retreat so the result does not depend on jurisdictional retreat rules.

**Authority notes:** Common-law self-defense doctrine

### orig-crim-016 — Conspiracy - withdrawal after the agreement but before substantive offenses

A woman and a man agreed to burglarize a warehouse the following night. The jurisdiction follows the common-law rule that conspiracy is complete upon agreement and requires no overt act. Several hours before the planned burglary, the woman told the man unequivocally that she was withdrawing and would not participate. She also gave the police enough information to prevent the crime. The police failed to act, and the man committed the burglary alone.

**Of which crimes may the woman be convicted?**

**(A)** The woman may not be convicted of either offense.
**(B)** The woman may be convicted of conspiracy, but not burglary.
**(C)** The woman may be convicted of burglary, but not conspiracy.
**(D)** The woman may be convicted of both conspiracy and burglary.

**Key:** B

**Rule:** At common law, conspiracy is complete upon agreement. Later withdrawal is not a defense to conspiracy, but timely affirmative withdrawal communicated to co-conspirators—and, when appropriate, efforts to thwart the crime—can terminate liability for subsequent offenses committed in furtherance of the conspiracy.

**Application:** The woman remained guilty of conspiracy because the agreement was complete. Her unequivocal withdrawal and report to police occurred before the burglary and cut off derivative liability for that later crime.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** Withdrawal does not undo a conspiracy that was already complete upon agreement. Thus she remains liable for conspiracy even though withdrawal can limit responsibility for later crimes.
- **B:** The conspiracy was complete when the parties agreed. The woman then took timely affirmative steps to withdraw and notify authorities, cutting off liability for the later burglary committed after withdrawal.
- **C:** The agreement established conspiracy under the stated common-law rule. Her effective withdrawal prevents liability for the later burglary rather than eliminating the conspiracy conviction.
- **D:** Her timely communication to the man and report to police were sufficient to terminate responsibility for the later burglary, even though the police failed to prevent it.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass expressly supplied the jurisdiction's no-overt-act rule, placed withdrawal before the burglary, and included both communication and police notification.

**Authority notes:** Common-law conspiracy doctrine; Pinkerton v. United States, 328 U.S. 640 (1946)

### orig-crim-017 — Fourth Amendment - consent by a co-occupant with common authority

Police officers went to an apartment shared by two roommates to investigate drug sales. One roommate answered the door. The other roommate was away and had not previously objected to a search. The roommate who answered told the officers that both tenants jointly used the kitchen and living room and consented to a search of those areas. In a kitchen cabinet used by both roommates, the officers found illegal drugs belonging to the absent roommate.

**Is the evidence admissible against the absent roommate?**

**(A)** No, because one occupant can never consent to a search that may produce evidence against another occupant.
**(B)** No, because the police needed a warrant once they suspected that the drugs belonged to the absent roommate.
**(C)** Yes, because the consenting roommate had common authority over the shared areas and cabinet.
**(D)** Yes, but only if the consenting roommate also owned the drugs found in the cabinet.

**Key:** C

**Rule:** A person who possesses common authority through mutual use and joint access or control may consent to a search of shared premises. That consent is generally valid against an absent co-occupant who has not contemporaneously objected.

**Application:** The consenting roommate jointly used the kitchen, living room, and cabinet. The officers confined the search to those common areas while the other roommate was absent.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** A person with common authority over shared premises may consent to a search of those common areas, and the consent is valid against an absent co-occupant.
- **B:** Valid third-party consent is an exception to the warrant requirement. Ownership of the seized item does not invalidate consent to search a shared location.
- **C:** Joint access and control creates authority to consent to a search of common areas. The absent roommate assumed the risk that the co-occupant would permit entry.
- **D:** Authority to consent turns on joint access or control over the place searched, not ownership of the evidence discovered there.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass confined the search to undeniably shared areas and stated that the absent occupant had not made a contemporaneous objection.

**Authority notes:** United States v. Matlock, 415 U.S. 164 (1974); Georgia v. Randolph, 547 U.S. 103 (2006); Fernandez v. California, 571 U.S. 292 (2014)

### orig-crim-018 — Miranda - questioning by an undercover agent

A robbery suspect was arrested on an unrelated charge and placed in a jail cell. Before any formal robbery charge had been filed, police placed an undercover officer in the cell. The suspect believed the officer was another detainee. During a casual conversation initiated by the undercover officer, the suspect voluntarily described how he had committed the robbery. The officer gave no Miranda warnings and used no threats or promises.

**Is the suspect's statement admissible in a later robbery prosecution?**

**(A)** No, because any questioning of a person who is physically in custody requires Miranda warnings.
**(B)** No, because the undercover officer deliberately elicited the incriminating statement.
**(C)** Yes, but only because the suspect was jailed on a charge unrelated to the robbery.
**(D)** Yes, because questioning by an undercover officer whom the suspect believes to be a fellow detainee does not require Miranda warnings.

**Key:** D

**Rule:** Miranda warnings are required for custodial interrogation, but not for questioning by an undercover agent when the suspect is unaware that the questioner is a law-enforcement officer, because the coercive pressures underlying Miranda are absent. The statement must still be voluntary.

**Application:** The suspect believed he was speaking casually with another detainee, no robbery charge had initiated the Sixth Amendment right, and no coercion made the statement involuntary.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** Miranda addresses custodial interrogation in a police-dominated setting. An unsuspecting conversation with an undercover agent does not create the coercive pressures Miranda is designed to dispel.
- **B:** Deliberate elicitation is relevant to the Sixth Amendment after adversarial proceedings begin. No robbery charge had been filed, and Miranda does not require warnings for undercover questioning of this kind.
- **C:** The absence of formal robbery charges matters to the Sixth Amendment, but the Miranda result rests on the lack of a coercive, known police interrogation. Undercover questioning can fall outside Miranda even when the subject matter is related to custody.
- **D:** Because the suspect did not know he was speaking to law enforcement, the exchange lacked the police-dominated coercive atmosphere that triggers Miranda protections. The statement was also voluntary.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass specified that no robbery charge had been filed and that the statement was voluntary, eliminating separate Sixth Amendment and due-process grounds for exclusion.

**Authority notes:** Illinois v. Perkins, 496 U.S. 292 (1990)

### orig-evidence-014 — Present sense impression - statement describing an event while observing it

In a negligence action arising from a collision, the plaintiff sought to prove that a blue sedan entered an intersection against a red light. A pedestrian standing at the intersection had sent a text message to a friend while watching the event: “A blue sedan is running the red light and heading north.” The pedestrian was unavailable at trial. The parties stipulated that the message was authentic and had been sent at the time shown on the phone.

**Is the text message admissible to prove how the sedan entered the intersection?**

**(A)** Yes, because it described the sedan's movement while the pedestrian was observing it.
**(B)** Yes, but only if the pedestrian was startled by the sedan.
**(C)** No, because the pedestrian is unavailable for cross-examination.
**(D)** No, because a written electronic message cannot qualify as a present sense impression.

**Key:** A

**Rule:** A statement describing or explaining an event or condition is admissible as a present sense impression if made while or immediately after the declarant perceived it. The exception applies regardless of availability.

**Application:** The pedestrian sent the authenticated text while watching the sedan run the light. Its content directly described the event as perceived.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** The pedestrian described the event while perceiving it. Rule 803(1) permits such a statement regardless of whether the declarant is available.
- **B:** A startling event and continued stress are requirements for an excited utterance, not for a present sense impression.
- **C:** The present-sense-impression exception applies regardless of the declarant's availability. In this civil case, the Confrontation Clause is not implicated.
- **D:** Rule 803(1) is not limited to oral statements. A contemporaneous text can qualify if properly authenticated and sufficiently contemporaneous.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass stipulated authentication and contemporaneous transmission so admissibility turns only on the hearsay exception.

**Authority notes:** Fed. R. Evid. 803(1)

### orig-evidence-015 — Former testimony - unavailable declarant and prior opportunity with similar motive

A pedestrian sued a bus company after being struck by one of its buses. After an eyewitness was diagnosed with a terminal illness, the parties took a videotaped deposition to preserve the witness's testimony for trial. The eyewitness stated that the bus entered the crosswalk after the traffic signal turned red. The bus company's lawyer fully cross-examined the witness about the signal, vantage point, and lighting conditions. The eyewitness died before trial, and the pedestrian offered the authenticated deposition against the bus company.

**Is the deposition testimony admissible?**

**(A)** No, because deposition testimony is hearsay whenever the witness cannot appear at trial.
**(B)** Yes, because the witness is unavailable and the bus company previously had an opportunity and similar motive to examine the witness.
**(C)** Yes, because every statement made under oath is excluded from the definition of hearsay.
**(D)** No, because the pedestrian, rather than the bus company, originally noticed the deposition.

**Key:** B

**Rule:** Former testimony is admissible if the declarant is unavailable, the testimony was given at a trial, hearing, or lawful deposition, and the party against whom it is offered had an opportunity and similar motive to develop the testimony by examination.

**Application:** The witness died, the testimony came from a lawful deposition, and the bus company conducted extensive cross-examination on the same disputed facts.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** Former testimony given at a lawful deposition may fall within Rule 804(b)(1) when the declarant is unavailable and the opposing party had an opportunity and similar motive to examine the witness.
- **B:** Death makes the eyewitness unavailable. The bus company cross-examined the eyewitness on the same factual issue and had a similar motive to undermine the testimony.
- **C:** An oath alone does not make a statement nonhearsay. The testimony is admissible under an exception requiring unavailability, opportunity, and similar motive.
- **D:** Who noticed the deposition is not dispositive. The key is that the party against whom the testimony is now offered had an opportunity and similar motive to examine the witness.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass made the deposition a trial-preservation deposition prompted by terminal illness and included full cross-examination on the identical traffic-signal issue, making similar motive especially clear.

**Authority notes:** Fed. R. Evid. 804(a)(4), 804(b)(1)

### orig-evidence-016 — Liability insurance - admissible to show witness bias

In a products-liability trial, the manufacturer called an investigator who testified that the product showed signs of misuse. On cross-examination, the plaintiff sought to establish that the investigator was a full-time employee of the manufacturer's liability insurer and had been assigned to investigate the plaintiff's claim. The manufacturer objected that the questioning would reveal the existence of liability insurance.

**Should the court permit the questioning?**

**(A)** No, because evidence of liability insurance is inadmissible for every purpose.
**(B)** No, because bias may be shown only through the witness's prior inconsistent statements.
**(C)** Yes, because the evidence is offered to show the investigator's possible bias rather than the manufacturer's fault.
**(D)** Yes, because the existence of insurance tends to prove that the manufacturer expected its products to cause injuries.

**Key:** C

**Rule:** Evidence that a person was or was not insured is inadmissible to prove negligence or wrongful conduct, but may be admitted for another purpose, including proving a witness's bias or prejudice.

**Application:** The plaintiff seeks to show that the investigator works for the insurer handling the claim, a relationship that may influence the investigator's testimony. The evidence is not offered to prove product defect.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** Rule 411 bars insurance evidence when offered to prove negligence or wrongful conduct, but expressly permits it for another relevant purpose such as proving bias or agency.
- **B:** Bias may be proved through relationships, employment, financial interests, or other evidence tending to show partiality; it is not limited to inconsistent statements.
- **C:** Employment by the insurer responsible for the claim can reveal a motive to favor the manufacturer. Rule 411 permits insurance evidence for that purpose, subject to ordinary Rule 403 balancing.
- **D:** That is precisely the forbidden propensity-like use of insurance to suggest fault or consciousness of risk. The proper purpose is witness bias.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass framed the evidence exclusively as employment-based bias and removed facts that might invite an improper inference of negligence.

**Authority notes:** Fed. R. Evid. 411; Fed. R. Evid. 403

### orig-evidence-017 — Original-writing rule - admissibility of a duplicate

In a federal breach-of-contract action, the plaintiff offered a clear photocopy of the parties' signed written agreement to prove its terms. A witness with knowledge established that the photocopy accurately reproduced the original. The defendant did not dispute the original's authenticity and identified no circumstance making use of the photocopy unfair, but objected solely because the plaintiff had not produced the original document.

**Should the court admit the photocopy?**

**(A)** No, because the original-writing rule always requires the original when a party seeks to prove a writing's terms.
**(B)** No, unless the plaintiff first proves that the original was lost or destroyed without bad faith.
**(C)** Yes, but only because the defendant admitted the contract's terms in the pleadings.
**(D)** Yes, because a duplicate is admissible absent a genuine question about authenticity or unfairness.

**Key:** D

**Rule:** A duplicate is admissible to the same extent as the original unless a genuine question is raised about the original's authenticity or the circumstances make admission of the duplicate unfair.

**Application:** The copy is accurate, authenticity is undisputed, and no unfairness is shown. The plaintiff need not prove that the original is lost before using the duplicate.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** The rules permit a duplicate to the same extent as an original unless a genuine authenticity question exists or admitting the duplicate would be unfair.
- **B:** Loss or destruction is relevant when a party offers other secondary evidence. A duplicate generally needs no preliminary showing that the original is unavailable.
- **C:** No such admission is stated or required. The accurate photocopy itself is admissible as a duplicate under Rule 1003.
- **D:** The photocopy accurately reproduces the original, and the defendant identifies neither an authenticity dispute nor unfair circumstances. Rule 1003 permits its admission.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass stipulated accuracy, removed authenticity and fairness concerns, and made the objection rest solely on the absence of the original.

**Authority notes:** Fed. R. Evid. 1001(e), 1003

### orig-evidence-018 — Prior identification - declarant testifies and is subject to cross-examination

At a robbery trial, an eyewitness testified that she could no longer remember the robber's face. She acknowledged that one week after the robbery she viewed a properly conducted photo array and selected the defendant's photograph. The eyewitness remained on the stand and was available for cross-examination. The prosecutor then called the detective who administered the array to testify that the eyewitness identified the defendant.

**Is the detective's testimony about the identification admissible for its truth?**

**(A)** Yes, because the eyewitness made a prior identification after perceiving the robber and now testifies subject to cross-examination.
**(B)** Yes, but only for impeachment because the eyewitness cannot presently remember the robber.
**(C)** No, because only the eyewitness may testify about her own prior identification.
**(D)** No, because the eyewitness's inability to remember the robber makes meaningful cross-examination impossible.

**Key:** A

**Rule:** A declarant-witness's prior statement identifying a person after perceiving that person is not hearsay if the declarant testifies and is subject to cross-examination about the statement. The identifier need not presently remember the event, and an observer may recount the identification.

**Application:** The eyewitness perceived the robber, selected the defendant soon afterward, took the stand, and remained available for cross-examination. The detective may therefore recount the identification for its truth.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** Rule 801(d)(1)(C) treats a qualifying prior identification as nonhearsay when the declarant testifies and is subject to cross-examination. Another witness may recount the identification.
- **B:** A qualifying prior identification is admissible substantively, not merely to impeach. Present memory loss does not prevent cross-examination for purposes of the rule.
- **C:** The rule does not limit proof to the declarant. A third person who observed the identification may testify to it if the declarant testifies and is subject to cross-examination.
- **D:** A witness who takes the stand and responds to questions is subject to cross-examination even if memory is impaired. The jury may evaluate the memory problem when weighing the identification.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass stated that the photo procedure was proper, kept the declarant on the stand, and used memory loss rather than total refusal to testify.

**Authority notes:** Fed. R. Evid. 801(d)(1)(C); United States v. Owens, 484 U.S. 554 (1988)

### orig-property-014 — Landlord-tenant - duty to mitigate after abandonment

A tenant leased an apartment for one year at $2,000 per month. After four months, the tenant moved out, returned the keys, and said that he would pay no further rent. The landlord promptly stated that it did not accept a surrender and retained the keys only to permit access for reletting. Comparable apartments in the building were renting quickly, but the landlord made no effort to advertise, show, or relet the apartment. The jurisdiction follows the modern majority rule. The landlord sued for the remaining $16,000.

**How much may the landlord recover?**

**(A)** The full $16,000, because a tenant's abandonment does not affect the obligation to pay rent.
**(B)** Only the rent the landlord would have lost despite making reasonable efforts to relet the apartment.
**(C)** Nothing, because returning the keys automatically terminated the lease and all future liability.
**(D)** Nothing, because the landlord's failure to relet constitutes acceptance of a surrender by operation of law.

**Key:** B

**Rule:** Under the modern majority rule, a landlord must make reasonable efforts to mitigate damages after a tenant abandons. The landlord may recover rent or damages that could not reasonably have been avoided, but not avoidable losses.

**Application:** The landlord did nothing despite strong demand for comparable units. Recovery is limited to any rent that would have remained unpaid even after reasonable reletting efforts.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** Under the modern majority rule, a landlord must make reasonable efforts to mitigate damages after a tenant abandons. Avoidable rental loss is not recoverable.
- **B:** The landlord may recover unavoidable loss but cannot recover rent that reasonable mitigation would have prevented. The failure to make any effort limits damages.
- **C:** Abandonment and return of keys do not automatically discharge the tenant. The tenant remains liable for damages, reduced by amounts the landlord reasonably could have avoided.
- **D:** Mere inaction is not necessarily acceptance of surrender. The dispositive modern rule is mitigation, which reduces rather than automatically eliminates damages.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass expressly adopted the modern majority rule, negated acceptance of surrender, and avoided stating how quickly this unit necessarily would have relet, so the answer properly describes the recoverable measure.

**Authority notes:** Modern majority landlord-tenant mitigation rule; Sommer v. Kridel, 378 A.2d 767 (N.J. 1977)

### orig-property-015 — Easements - use to benefit after-acquired land

The owner of Parcel A held an express easement across neighboring Parcel B for access from Parcel A to a public road. The easement was appurtenant to Parcel A and did not mention any other land. The owner later purchased Parcel C, which bordered Parcel A but not Parcel B. He built a driveway from Parcel C across Parcel A and began using the easement over Parcel B to reach both parcels. The additional traffic caused no measurable physical damage to Parcel B.

**May the owner of Parcel B enjoin use of the easement to serve Parcel C?**

**(A)** No, because the additional use caused no measurable physical damage to Parcel B.
**(B)** No, because Parcel C became part of the dominant estate when its owner also acquired Parcel A.
**(C)** Yes, because an easement appurtenant to Parcel A ordinarily may not be used to benefit after-acquired Parcel C.
**(D)** Yes, but only if the traffic from Parcel C substantially increases the burden on Parcel B.

**Key:** C

**Rule:** An easement appurtenant ordinarily may be used only for the benefit of the identified dominant estate. The dominant owner generally may not extend it to serve after-acquired land, even if the added burden is modest.

**Application:** The grant benefited only Parcel A. Routing Parcel C traffic through Parcel A and over Parcel B uses the easement for land outside the dominant estate.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** Use of an appurtenant easement to benefit after-acquired land is generally impermissible even if the increased burden is slight and causes no proven damage.
- **B:** Common ownership does not automatically enlarge the dominant estate described in the easement. Parcel C remains after-acquired land outside the original grant.
- **C:** The easement was created to serve Parcel A. Extending its use to Parcel C exceeds the authorized scope and may be enjoined by the servient owner.
- **D:** A substantial increase may independently violate scope limits, but using an easement for land outside the dominant estate is generally misuse even without a substantial added burden.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass specified an appurtenant easement, identified the original dominant parcel, and stated that Parcel C was acquired later, making after-acquired-land misuse the sole issue.

**Authority notes:** Common-law easement scope doctrine; Restatement (Third) of Property: Servitudes § 4.11

### orig-property-016 — Statute of frauds - part performance of an oral land-sale contract

A landowner orally agreed to sell a farm to a buyer for $500,000. **(D)** Yes, because payment, possession, and substantial improvements constitute sufficient part performance.

### orig-property-017 — Transfer by mortgagee - mortgage follows the transferred debt

A homeowner borrowed money from a lender and signed a promissory note secured by a mortgage on her home. The lender later validly transferred the promissory note to an investor but executed no separate written assignment of the mortgage. The homeowner received notice of the transfer and then defaulted. The investor sought to foreclose the mortgage. No recording-act issue is involved.

**May the investor foreclose?**

**(A)** Yes, because a valid transfer of the secured debt ordinarily carries the mortgage with it.
**(B)** Yes, but only because the homeowner received notice of the transfer before defaulting.
**(C)** No, because a mortgage can be transferred only by a separately executed and recorded assignment.
**(D)** No, because transferring the note without the mortgage extinguished the mortgage lien.

**Key:** A

**Rule:** A mortgage is generally an incident of the obligation it secures. A valid transfer of the note or debt ordinarily carries the mortgage with it, even without a separate assignment of the mortgage.

**Application:** The investor validly acquired the promissory note. Because the mortgage secures that debt and no competing recording issue exists, the investor may enforce the mortgage upon default.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** The mortgage is an incident of the debt. Transfer of the note generally transfers the beneficial interest in the mortgage even without a separate mortgage assignment.
- **B:** Notice may affect whom the homeowner should pay, but the mortgage follows a valid transfer of the debt independently of notice.
- **C:** A separate assignment may be useful for recording and proof, but the general rule is that the mortgage follows the note. Recording is not necessary between these parties in the stated circumstances.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass removed negotiability and recording complications and expressly stated that the note transfer was valid and no recording-act issue exists.

### orig-property-018 — General warranty deed - present covenant of seisin

A seller conveyed a tract to a buyer by general warranty deed. At the time of conveyance, a five-foot strip along the eastern boundary was actually owned by a neighbor, although neither the seller nor the buyer knew that fact. The buyer took possession of the entire tract, and the neighbor had not yet demanded possession or otherwise disturbed the buyer. The buyer later discovered the title defect and immediately sued the seller for breach of a deed covenant.

**Which covenant provides the buyer with an immediate claim?**

**(A)** The covenant of quiet enjoyment, because the buyer's title is imperfect.
**(B)** The covenant of seisin, because the seller did not own the entire estate purportedly conveyed.
**(C)** The covenant of warranty, because any undisclosed title defect breaches that covenant at delivery.
**(D)** No covenant, because the neighbor has not yet dispossessed the buyer.

**Key:** B

**Rule:** The covenant of seisin is a present covenant that the grantor owns the estate the deed purports to convey. It is breached, if at all, upon delivery. Covenants of quiet enjoyment and warranty are future covenants breached upon disturbance by superior title.

**Application:** The seller lacked title to the five-foot strip at delivery, so the covenant of seisin was immediately breached even though the neighbor has not yet evicted the buyer.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** Quiet enjoyment is a future covenant and is breached only upon actual or constructive eviction by a holder of superior title, not merely upon discovery of a defect.
- **B:** Seisin is a present covenant that the grantor owns the estate described in the deed. It was breached at delivery because the neighbor owned the strip.
- **C:** Warranty is a future covenant promising defense against lawful claims of superior title. It is not breached until the grantee is disturbed or evicted.
- **D:** The absence of eviction prevents a present claim on future covenants but does not prevent an immediate claim for breach of the present covenant of seisin.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass made the neighbor's superior ownership undisputed and specified no disturbance, isolating seisin from the future covenants.

**Authority notes:** Common-law covenants for title

### orig-torts-014 — False imprisonment - intentional confinement without reasonable means of escape

A store detective saw a customer pay for every item in her shopping bag. Hoping to pressure the customer into admitting to an unrelated theft, the detective directed her into a small office, locked the only door, and said that she could not leave until the police arrived. The customer repeatedly asked to leave and remained confined for 20 minutes. The detective had no reasonable basis to believe that the customer had stolen anything.

**Is the detective liable to the customer for false imprisonment?**

**(A)** No, because confinement lasting only 20 minutes is too brief to support false imprisonment.
**(B)** No, because merchants are absolutely privileged to detain customers while investigating suspected theft.
**(C)** Yes, because the detective intentionally confined the customer without consent or privilege.
**(D)** Yes, but only if the customer suffered physical injury during the confinement.

**Key:** C

**Rule:** False imprisonment is intentional confinement within fixed boundaries, without lawful privilege, when the plaintiff is aware of the confinement or harmed by it. A merchant may detain a suspected shoplifter only on reasonable grounds and in a reasonable manner for a reasonable time.

**Application:** The detective knowingly locked the customer in an office, the customer was aware and objected, and the detective had no reasonable basis for suspicion. The confinement was therefore unprivileged.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** False imprisonment does not require confinement for a minimum period. Even brief intentional confinement may be actionable.
- **B:** The shopkeeper's privilege is limited to reasonable detention based on reasonable grounds and conducted in a reasonable manner and time. Those requirements are absent here.
- **C:** Locking the only door intentionally confined a conscious, objecting customer. With no reasonable basis for suspected theft, the detention was not privileged.
- **D:** A plaintiff who is aware of the confinement need not prove physical injury. Awareness or resulting harm is sufficient.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass established awareness, a locked sole exit, lack of consent, and lack of reasonable grounds, removing disputes about escape or shopkeeper privilege.

**Authority notes:** Restatement (Second) of Torts §§ 35, 120A

### orig-torts-015 — Necessity - private necessity and liability for actual damage

While sailing on a lake, a boat owner was caught in a sudden and unusually violent storm. To prevent the boat from sinking and to protect the passengers, the owner tied the boat to a privately owned dock without the dock owner's permission. Remaining on the lake would have created a serious risk of death. During the storm, the boat repeatedly struck the dock and caused $4,000 in damage, despite the boat owner's reasonable care.

**What liability does the boat owner have to the dock owner?**

**(A)** Liability for trespass and punitive damages, because the boat owner intentionally entered private property.
**(B)** No liability, because necessity is a complete privilege against both entry and resulting property damage.
**(C)** Liability for the damage only if the boat owner failed to use reasonable care after tying up.
**(D)** No liability for the privileged entry, but liability for the $4,000 in actual damage to the dock.

**Key:** D

**Rule:** Private necessity privileges an intentional entry reasonably necessary to protect the actor, others, or property from serious harm. The privilege is incomplete, so the actor ordinarily must pay for actual damage caused to the property used.

**Application:** The dangerous storm made use of the dock reasonably necessary to protect lives and the boat. The owner is not liable for trespass but must pay for the physical damage caused.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** Private necessity privileges the entry against trespass liability and punitive damages when the intrusion is reasonably necessary to protect persons or property.
- **B:** Private necessity is incomplete: it protects the actor from trespass liability but ordinarily requires payment for actual damage caused.
- **C:** Even an actor who exercises reasonable care while acting under private necessity is generally strictly responsible for actual damage to the property used.
- **D:** The emergency justified use of the dock, so the entry was privileged. Because the necessity was private rather than public, the boat owner must compensate the dock owner for actual damage.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass made the emergency severe, the entry necessary, and the owner careful, ensuring that liability rests on the incomplete privilege rather than negligence.

**Authority notes:** Restatement (Second) of Torts §§ 197, 263; Vincent v. Lake Erie Transportation Co., 124 N.W. 221 (Minn. 1910)

### orig-torts-016 — Negligence per se - protected class and type of harm

A state statute required commercial trucks parked on a highway shoulder after sunset to display illuminated rear marker lights. The statute was enacted to protect motorists and bicyclists from nighttime collisions with trucks that are difficult to see. Without excuse, a truck driver parked on a dark shoulder after sunset and failed to turn on the marker lights. A bicyclist exercising reasonable care could not see the truck in time, struck it, and was injured.

**What effect does the statutory violation have in the bicyclist's negligence action?**

**(A)** It establishes breach through negligence per se, subject to proof of causation and damages.
**(B)** It is irrelevant because traffic statutes cannot define the standard of care in a tort action.
**(C)** It makes the driver strictly liable for every injury occurring while the truck was parked.
**(D)** It is evidence of negligence only, because a statutory violation can never establish breach as a matter of law.

**Key:** A

**Rule:** An unexcused violation of a statute may establish breach when the plaintiff is among the class the statute was designed to protect and suffers the type of harm the statute was designed to prevent. Causation and damages remain required.

**Application:** The statute protects road users such as the bicyclist from nighttime collisions with unlit parked trucks. The driver violated it without excuse, and that risk materialized.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** The bicyclist is within the class the statute protects, the collision is the type of harm the statute seeks to prevent, and the violation was unexcused.
- **B:** A safety statute may supply the standard of care when it protects the plaintiff's class from the type of harm suffered.
- **C:** Negligence per se establishes duty and breach, not automatic liability. The plaintiff must still prove actual and proximate causation and damages.
- **D:** Under the negligence-per-se doctrine, an unexcused violation of a qualifying safety statute generally establishes breach, although some jurisdictions treat violations only as evidence. The MBE applies the generally accepted negligence-per-se rule.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass matched the protected class and harm precisely, expressly negated excuse, and preserved causation and damages as separate elements.

**Authority notes:** Restatement (Second) of Torts §§ 286, 288A

### orig-torts-017 — Vicarious liability - minor detour within the scope of employment

A delivery company employed a driver to deliver packages along a designated city route. While making deliveries, the driver traveled two blocks off the route to buy coffee for himself. After buying the coffee, he was driving back toward the route to continue the deliveries when his negligent driving injured a pedestrian. The company had not prohibited brief personal stops during the workday.

**Is the delivery company vicariously liable for the pedestrian's injury?**

**(A)** No, because any personal purpose removes an employee from the scope of employment.
**(B)** Yes, because the brief coffee stop was a minor detour during an ongoing delivery route.
**(C)** No, because an employer is vicariously liable only when it specifically authorizes the employee's precise conduct.
**(D)** Yes, but only if the company itself was negligent in hiring or supervising the driver.

**Key:** B

**Rule:** An employer is vicariously liable for an employee's tort committed within the scope of employment. A minor personal deviation is a detour that ordinarily remains within scope, whereas a substantial departure is a frolic outside scope.

**Application:** The driver departed only two blocks for a brief coffee purchase and was returning to the delivery route when the accident occurred. The employment relationship remained sufficiently connected to the trip.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** A slight deviation for a personal purpose is ordinarily a detour that remains within the scope of employment; only a substantial abandonment of the employer's business is a frolic.
- **B:** The deviation was short in distance and duration, and the driver was returning to continue assigned deliveries. A factfinder would treat it as within the scope of employment.
- **C:** Scope of employment does not require authorization of the particular negligent act or every minor detail of the employee's conduct.
- **D:** Direct negligence by the employer is a separate basis of liability. Respondeat superior imposes vicarious liability for an employee's tort within the scope of employment without employer fault.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass supplied distance, duration, return-to-route facts, and no contrary employer rule, making the detour characterization strong without eliminating realistic distractors.

**Authority notes:** Restatement (Third) of Agency §§ 7.07, 7.08

### orig-torts-018 — Private nuisance - unusually sensitive use of land

A factory operated in compliance with all applicable regulations and emitted a faint low-frequency hum. The hum was not audible inside ordinary nearby homes and did not interfere with ordinary residential activities. A neighbor used a room in his home as an unusually sensitive professional sound-recording studio. The hum was detectable by his specialized equipment and made some recordings unusable. The factory knew about the studio only after the neighbor complained.

**Is the factory likely liable to the neighbor for private nuisance?**

**(A)** Yes, because any physical effect from one parcel that reduces the value of another parcel is a nuisance.
**(B)** Yes, because regulatory compliance is a complete admission that the factory knowingly caused the interference.
**(C)** No, because the interference affects only an unusually sensitive use and would not substantially disturb an ordinary land user.
**(D)** No, because compliance with regulations always precludes nuisance liability.

**Key:** C

**Rule:** Private nuisance requires a substantial and unreasonable interference with another's use or enjoyment of land. Substantiality is judged by the effect on an ordinary person or ordinary use, not by a plaintiff's unusually sensitive activity.

**Application:** The hum does not affect ordinary residences and is detected only by specialized recording equipment. The studio's exceptional sensitivity does not ordinarily convert the hum into a substantial nuisance.

**Choice rationales:**

- **A:** Private nuisance requires a substantial and unreasonable interference with use or enjoyment, not merely any measurable effect or economic loss.
- **B:** Compliance is relevant but not dispositive, and it is certainly not an admission of unreasonable interference. The hypersensitive nature of the neighbor's use is central.
- **C:** Nuisance protection is measured against ordinary use and sensibilities. An interference perceptible only to specialized equipment in a hypersensitive activity ordinarily is not substantial for nuisance purposes.
- **D:** Regulatory compliance does not categorically defeat nuisance liability. A substantial and unreasonable interference may still be actionable despite compliance.

**Second-pass note:** Second pass separated the hypersensitivity issue from regulatory compliance and stated that ordinary homes and activities were unaffected.

**Authority notes:** Restatement (Second) of Torts §§ 821D, 821F
